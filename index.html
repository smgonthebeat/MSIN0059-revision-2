1. Overlaps Between the 2021 Exam Paper and the Sample Exam Paper

By comparing the 2021 exam with the original sample exam ("Lecture 10 - Sample Exam Paper - without solutions.pdf"), the following topics and question‐styles appear in both papers:

  1. Cost Classification & Its Role in Managerial Decisions
    • 2021 Exam: A1(a) directly discusses the meaning of costs in various contexts.
    • Sample Exam: Often references how "contribution vs. full cost" or "direct vs. indirect cost" plays into decision making (Section A in the sample exam).
  2. Variance Analysis and Budgeting
    • 2021 Exam: A1(b) specifically addresses the nature of variances and how to interpret "favourable variance." A2 also includes part (b) about budgeting (cash budget for 6 months).
    • Sample Exam: Multiple parts talk about "All variances should be investigated," "flexing the budget," standard cost variances (e.g., in Section A, A1(b)–(d), also in A2 for the sample exam).
  3. Capital Investment Appraisal (NPV, Payback, Possibly IRR)
    • 2021 Exam: A2 requires net present value (NPV), payback period; B1 compares payback to IRR.
    • Sample Exam: Section A's second big question (A2) also involves NPV with a multi-year project, plus discount tables, cost of capital, residual values, etc.
  4. Budgeting Systems, Behavioral Implications, and Motivational Issues
    • 2021 Exam: A2(b) (the second question in A2) references managers' targets, staff motivation, and bonuses. Section B2 also focuses on the role of budgets, limiting factors, etc.
    • Sample Exam: Section B1 is all about the nature, purposes, and behavioral implications of budgeting systems.
  5. Standard Costing & Variance Reconciliation
    • 2021 Exam: Section C1 is explicitly about standard cost variance analysis, reconciling budgeted vs. actual profit.
    • Sample Exam: Variation of standard costing/variance analysis in sections A1(b)(c)(d) and A2(C) or (D).
  6. Break‐Even / CVP Analysis
    • 2021 Exam: Section C2 is a dedicated break-even question (calculating contribution margin, margin of safety, new branch, etc.).
    • Sample Exam: CVP was also fundamental to "contribution" and "marginal costing" discussions (Week 4 in your notes). The sample exam doesn't have a big dedicated break-even question but does test marginal costing in short‐term decisions, so the principle is repeated.

Thus, the common repeated themes across both exams—and hence crucial for your revision—are:

  • Cost classification and relevance (e.g., which costs matter for decisions).
  • NPV / capital investment calculations.
  • Budgeting and variance analysis (including the nature of "favourable/adverse" variances).
  • Behavioral aspects of budgeting (motivation, target‐setting).
  • Standard costing (material, labour, overhead variances).
  • Marginal costing, CVP, and break-even considerations.

These repeated items do show up in both papers. So you'll want to prioritize mastering them for your real exam.

"Honest Diamonds Ltd (HD) is considering investing in a new project to extract diamonds from a newly found source located in caves in Australia. This will give merchants the ability to have one 25 carat diamond block which they can refine into their customers' unique preferences. Estimates of revenues and costs arising from this project are:

Head office costs (£m) -0.9 -0.9 -0.9 -0.9
Inspection Costs (£m) -1.8
Interest payments (£m) -3 -3 -3 -3

If the project goes ahead, sales of other existing products will be lost resulting in a loss of contribution of £15 million a year, over the life of the project. Question A2 (Part B):

Hospitablet Ltd manufactures small electronic scanning tablets for use in medical institutions. It is starting to prepare its budget for the six months commencing 1st July 2022. In each month in recent times, the company has sold 60 tablets per month at a price of £4,000 each. Tablets purchased by the company from its supplier cost £2,500 each. The company anticipates that at the beginning of July 2022, it will have an overdraft of £90,000. The company has just been advised that a new model of tablet has just been released and they will be receiving this latest model. This new model has 1.5 times more processing power than the current model. The purchase cost from the supplier is expected to increase by 10%, and the company has decided to sell these new tablets for 15% more than the current model is sold for. From the beginning of July, sales are expected to increase consistently at a rate of 30 machines each month through until the end of December 2022. Monthly operating costs in recent times have been £6,000 but it is anticipated that this amount will increase to £7,000 for July 2022, followed by additional increases in subsequent months. Operating costs are forecast as follows:

July August September October November December
Operating costs 7,000 10,000 13,000 15,000 15,000 15,000

Note: These costs include depreciation of £3,000 a month.

Payment from customers are always on cash basis, and cash is received in the month of sales. The suppliers of the tablets have not been offering credit to the company yet, but will offer one (1) month's credit with purchases in October 2022 and thereafter. Operating costs are payable in the following month. As a result of growing concern regarding potential cyber-attacks, a new security software will be bought in and installed in September 2022, costing £150,000. A tax bill of £550,000 will be received at the end of December 2022, and is expected to be paid two (2) months later. Hospitablet plans to offer a £10,000 cash bonus to each of their ten (10) senior staff in September to thank them for their hard work and another successful year.

Required: a) Prepare a cash budget for the six months ending 31st December 2022 which shows the monthly cash flows and the cash balance at the end of each month. Show your calculations and answer to zero (0) decimal places. (6 marks) b) Looking further ahead, for the 2023 budget, the Chief Executive Officer (CEO) has asked the Chief Financial Officer (CFO) to set extremely challenging targets for senior managers to further motivate them. The CEO has stated that "this will keep staff working hard, whilst reducing the number of bonuses given out". Comment on the implications of this approach, and any suggestions that could help the CFO. (7 marks)"

Topics from Your Note

  1. Capital Budgeting: NPV, discounting, payback period, relevant/irrelevant costs (sunk inspection fee, lost contribution, overheads, environment restoration, working capital, etc.).
  2. Short-Term Budgeting: monthly cash budgets, changes in sales volume and unit cost, timing of payments/receipts, operating cost patterns.
  3. Behavioral Aspects: extremely challenging targets → potential motivational or dysfunctional outcomes.

Key Excerpts from Your Note

Week 6 (Relevant Costing): "When considering a proposed course of action, not all costs are relevant. Sunk costs (like older inspection fees) do not vary with the decision, so they should be excluded. Opportunity cost (lost contribution) should be included if that resource or time is redeployed."

Week 7 (CAPEX: NPV, Payback): "NPV: sum of the present values of future net cash flows minus the initial outlay… If NPV > 0, the project meets or exceeds the required rate of return… Payback: how long it takes for the cash investment to be returned by net inflows. It does not consider the time value of money. Working capital is typically recovered at the end of the project… Restoration or disposal costs at the end of a project must be accounted for in the relevant year's cash flows. … The cost of capital is used to discount future cash flows to their present values."

Week 8 (Budgeting): "Cash budget: we record inflows (sales receipts) and outflows (purchases, operating costs, tax, etc.) for each month. Payment timing is crucial, e.g., if suppliers offer credit from a certain month. Behavioral: extremely high targets can lead to demotivation or short-term 'gaming' of results. Conversely, too lenient targets create slack. Balanced, 'challenging but achievable' budgets often yield the best motivation."

Section B

Question B1 (Part A):

"'Payback is a better capital investment appraisal method than the Internal Rate of Return.' Required: Critique the statement immediately above. In so doing explain the nature and purpose of each of the two (2) methods referred to, and contrast them in terms of relative strengths and weaknesses."

Topics from Your Note

  • Capital Appraisal Methods: payback vs. IRR.
  • Strengths/weaknesses of each (time value of money, post-payback cash flows, multiple IRRs, etc.). Payback is simple but ignores time value of money and post-payback cash flows. IRR accounts for the time value of money but can be misleading if multiple sign changes exist.

Key Excerpts from Your Note

Week 7 "Payback: how long it takes for the cash investment to be recovered. No consideration of time value or post-payback returns. IRR: the discount rate at which NPV=0. It does consider time value. However, multiple IRRs can arise with unconventional cash flows. NPV is typically considered the best method, though IRR is still more sophisticated than payback in factoring in discounting."

Question B1 (Part B):

"You overhear an entrepreneur say to their accountant: 'In order to make the highest profit, it is necessary to outsource as much as possible, and the full costing method is very appropriate when determining the outsourcing cost. Also, full costing is better suited than marginal analysis for dealing with qualitative costs and benefits... so the answer always lies in the full cost.' Required: Critique the statement immediately above. In so doing, explain the nature of outsourcing in the contexts of Full Costing and Cost Volume Profit analysis."

Topics from Your Note

  • Outsourcing: whether to produce internally vs. buy externally.
  • Full (absorption) Costing vs. Marginal Costing: short-run relevant costs vs. overhead allocations, plus intangible factors (quality, supplier reliability).

Key Excerpts from Your Note

Week 4 (Marginal vs. Full Costing) "Marginal costing only includes variable costs in product costs. Full costing includes both variable and fixed costs. For outsourcing decisions, we need to consider which costs would be avoided if we outsourced (relevant costs). Fixed costs that would continue regardless should be excluded from the decision. Qualitative factors (quality control, reliability, strategic importance) must be considered alongside the quantitative analysis."

Week 6 (Relevant Costing) "For make-or-buy decisions, we compare the relevant costs of internal production with the external purchase price. Allocated overheads that would continue regardless are not relevant. The statement that 'full costing is better for qualitative factors' is incorrect—qualitative factors must be considered separately from either costing method."

Question B2:

"'Budgets are a waste of time. They are based on guesswork, and they are always wrong. They are also a tool for senior managers to control junior managers, and they are a source of conflict. They are not worth the effort.' Required: Critique the statement immediately above. In so doing, identify the need and drivers for, and the role of a budget in an organisation, and describe the process of setting up a budget."

Topics from Your Note

  • Purposes of Budgeting: planning, coordination, communication, control, motivation.
  • Behavioral Aspects: potential for conflict, gaming, but also benefits of goal alignment.
  • Budgeting Approaches: traditional vs. alternative approaches (e.g., rolling forecasts, beyond budgeting).

Key Excerpts from Your Note

Week 8 (Budgeting) "Budgets serve multiple purposes: planning, coordination, communication, control, motivation, and performance evaluation. While forecasts involve uncertainty, they are not mere 'guesswork' but informed estimates based on historical data, market research, and strategic plans.

Behavioral issues: Budgets can create tension between control and motivation. They can lead to budget gaming, building slack, or short-termism. However, when implemented well, they align individual goals with organizational objectives.

Alternative approaches: Beyond Budgeting advocates more adaptive processes. Rolling forecasts provide more current information. Activity-Based Budgeting links resources to activities and outputs."

Section C

Question C1:

"Mango Ltd manufactures a single product. The standard cost card for one unit of the product is as follows:

Direct materials: 5 kg at £8 per kg £40
Direct labour: 3 hours at £12 per hour £36
Variable production overhead: 3 hours at £4 per hour £12
Fixed production overhead: 3 hours at £6 per hour £18
Total standard cost £106

Budgeted production and sales for April 2021 were 5,000 units. The budgeted selling price was £150 per unit. The actual results for April 2021 were as follows:

Production 4,800 units
Sales 4,600 units at £152 per unit
Direct materials purchased and used 25,000 kg at £7.80 per kg
Direct labour 15,000 hours at £11.80 per hour
Variable production overhead £58,000
Fixed production overhead £92,000

Required: a) Calculate the following variances for April 2021:

  1. Sales price variance (2 marks)
  2. Sales volume profit variance (2 marks)
  3. Direct material price variance (2 marks)
  4. Direct material usage variance (2 marks)
  5. Direct labour rate variance (2 marks)
  6. Direct labour efficiency variance (2 marks)
  7. Variable production overhead expenditure variance (2 marks)
  8. Variable production overhead efficiency variance (2 marks)
  9. Fixed production overhead expenditure variance (2 marks)
  10. Fixed production overhead volume variance (2 marks)

b) Prepare a statement that reconciles the budgeted profit with the actual profit for April 2021. (5 marks)

Topics from Your Note

  • Standard Costing: setting standards for materials, labor, and overheads.
  • Variance Analysis: calculating and interpreting price/rate, usage/efficiency, and volume variances.
  • Reconciliation: connecting budgeted profit to actual profit via variances.

Key Excerpts from Your Note

Week 9 (Standard Costing & Variance Analysis) "Standard costing establishes predetermined costs for products. Variances measure deviations from these standards:

  • Material price variance = (Standard price – Actual price) × Actual quantity
  • Material usage variance = (Standard quantity – Actual quantity) × Standard price
  • Labour rate variance = (Standard rate – Actual rate) × Actual hours
  • Labour efficiency variance = (Standard hours – Actual hours) × Standard rate
  • Variable overhead expenditure variance = (Standard rate – Actual rate) × Actual hours
  • Variable overhead efficiency variance = (Standard hours – Actual hours) × Standard rate
  • Fixed overhead expenditure variance = Budgeted fixed overhead – Actual fixed overhead
  • Fixed overhead volume variance = (Actual production – Budgeted production) × Standard fixed overhead rate per unit
  • Sales price variance = (Actual price – Standard price) × Actual quantity
  • Sales volume profit variance = (Actual quantity – Budgeted quantity) × Standard profit per unit

Reconciliation: Budgeted profit + Favourable variances – Adverse variances = Actual profit"

Question C2:

"Pear Ltd manufactures and sells a single product. The following information relates to the company's activities:

Selling price per unit £100
Variable cost per unit £60
Fixed costs per month £80,000

Required: a) Calculate the following:

  1. Contribution per unit (1 mark)
  2. Contribution to sales ratio (1 mark)
  3. Break-even point in units (2 marks)
  4. Break-even point in sales value (2 marks)
  5. Margin of safety in units, if the company expects to sell 3,000 units per month (2 marks)
  6. Margin of safety as a percentage of expected sales (2 marks)

b) The company is considering opening a new branch. The new branch would incur additional fixed costs of £30,000 per month. The selling price and variable cost per unit would remain unchanged. Calculate the revised break-even point in units for the company as a whole. (3 marks)

c) The company is considering a proposal to increase the selling price to £110 per unit. It is estimated that this would result in a 10% reduction in the number of units sold. Calculate the effect of this proposal on the company's monthly profit, assuming that the company currently sells 3,000 units per month. (5 marks)

d) The company is considering a proposal to reduce the selling price to £90 per unit. It is estimated that this would result in a 20% increase in the number of units sold. Calculate the effect of this proposal on the company's monthly profit, assuming that the company currently sells 3,000 units per month. (5 marks)

e) The company is considering a proposal to spend an additional £10,000 per month on advertising. It is estimated that this would result in a 15% increase in the number of units sold. Calculate the effect of this proposal on the company's monthly profit, assuming that the company currently sells 3,000 units per month. (5 marks)

Topics from Your Note

  • Cost-Volume-Profit (CVP) Analysis: contribution margin, break-even point, margin of safety.
  • Sensitivity Analysis: impact of changes in price, volume, fixed costs on profit.
  • Decision Making: evaluating alternative scenarios (price changes, advertising investment).

Key Excerpts from Your Note

Week 4 (CVP Analysis) "Contribution = Selling price – Variable cost per unit

Contribution to sales ratio = (Contribution per unit ÷ Selling price) × 100%

Break-even point (units) = Fixed costs ÷ Contribution per unit

Break-even point (sales value) = Fixed costs ÷ Contribution to sales ratio

Margin of safety (units) = Expected sales (units) – Break-even sales (units)

Margin of safety (%) = (Margin of safety in units ÷ Expected sales in units) × 100%

When evaluating changes (price, volume, fixed costs), we recalculate the contribution and compare the new profit with the original profit."

5. Summary Boxes

Box 1: Marginal vs. Absorption Costing

ASPECT MARGINAL COSTING ABSORPTION COSTING
Definition Only variable costs are charged to products; fixed costs are period expenses. Both variable and fixed production costs are assigned to units.
Fixed Costs Treatment Expensed in the period incurred. Included in inventory cost and expensed when sold.
Usefulness Helpful for short-term decisions: shutdowns, limiting factor analysis, make-or-buy, pricing. Required for external reporting under accounting standards.
Managerial Decision-Making Provides clear contribution margin. May distort profitability when production ≠ sales.
Inventory Valuation Impact Lower profit when inventory increases. Higher profit when inventory increases (fixed OH deferred).
Limitations Ignores long-term sustainability (total cost recovery). May lead to dysfunctional decisions (e.g., overproduction to increase profit).

📚 Section(s): A1, B2, C1, C2

Box 2: Cost Classification & Relevance

COST TYPE DEFINITION RELEVANCE TO DECISIONS
Direct Costs Can be traced to specific products/services Relevant for product costing and pricing
Indirect Costs Cannot be directly traced to products Allocation needed; may be irrelevant for short-term decisions
Fixed Costs Don't change with activity level Often irrelevant for short-term decisions unless avoidable
Variable Costs Change proportionally with activity Usually relevant for most decisions
Sunk Costs Already incurred; cannot be recovered Always irrelevant for future decisions
Opportunity Costs Value of foregone alternatives Relevant but not recorded in accounting systems

Box 3: Capital Investment Appraisal Methods

METHOD DESCRIPTION ADVANTAGES DISADVANTAGES
Net Present Value (NPV) Sum of discounted future cash flows minus initial investment Considers time value of money; accounts for all cash flows More complex calculations; requires accurate discount rate
Payback Period Time to recover initial investment Simple to calculate; focuses on liquidity Ignores time value of money; ignores post-payback cash flows
Internal Rate of Return (IRR) Discount rate at which NPV equals zero Considers time value; gives percentage return Multiple IRRs possible; may conflict with NPV for mutually exclusive projects
Accounting Rate of Return Average profit ÷ Average investment Uses familiar accounting measures Ignores time value; based on profit not cash flow

VARIANCE TYPE FORMULA INTERPRETATION

Break-Even Point (units) Fixed Costs ÷ Contribution per unit Volume needed to cover all costs (zero profit)
Break-Even Point (value) Fixed Costs ÷ Contribution ratio Sales value needed to cover all costs
Contribution Margin Selling price − Variable cost per unit Amount each unit contributes to fixed costs and profit
Contribution Ratio Contribution ÷ Sales × 100% Percentage of sales that contributes to fixed costs and profit
Margin of Safety (Current sales − Break-even sales) ÷ Current sales × 100% How far sales can fall before losses occur
Target Profit Volume (Fixed costs + Target profit) ÷ Contribution per unit Volume needed to achieve desired profit
